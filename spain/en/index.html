Spain

Backpacking Spain

Most people picture Spain before they've ever set foot in it — sangria, siestas, a beach towel reserved with a paperback at 7am. Some of that is real. Most of it is the tourist-brochure version of a country that's actually stranger, older, and considerably more fun than the postcards let on. This is a country where an entire city runs from live bulls once a year for sport, where a flamenco dancer can silence a packed bar with a single stamp of a heel, and where you can be hiking a snow-capped mountain range in the morning and swimming in the Mediterranean by evening. Forget the sun lounger — this is Spain built for people who want a genuinely big adventure, not just a week off.

If you're coming from the UK or anywhere else in Western Europe, Spain will feel closer than it is — close enough that the flight barely counts as travel, different enough that within a day you'll wonder how somewhere this near ever felt this foreign. Life runs on a different clock here: lunch stretches past 2pm, dinner doesn't start until 10, and the streets are still full of families and pensioners at midnight. Head to Galicia in the rain-soaked northwest and it barely resembles the Spain of the brochures — all green hills, seafood, and Celtic folk music. Head south to Andalusia and you're somewhere that feels half Spanish, half Moroccan, whitewashed hill towns and horseshoe arches wherever you look.

And then there's what it costs, which is the part most first-timers can't quite believe. A proper three-course lunch with wine — the menú del día, a genuine Spanish institution — will often set you back less than a meal deal at a UK service station. Dorm beds, intercity buses, and a round of beers are all priced like it's still a decade ago. Whatever adventure you had in mind, Spain will let you have a bigger one for less than you budgeted.

About Spain

Spain sits on the Iberian Peninsula in southwestern Europe, sharing its landmass with Portugal and separated from North Africa by just 14km of water at the Strait of Gibraltar. It is the fourth-largest country in Europe by area, and one of the most geographically varied: snow-capped mountains in the Pyrenees and the Sierra Nevada, a green and rainy Atlantic coast in the north, a bone-dry central plateau (the Meseta), and Mediterranean and Atlantic coastlines fringed with some of Europe's best beaches. The population is around 48 million, concentrated heavily in Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, and Seville, with vast, sparsely populated interior regions in between — Spain is, on average, one of the emptiest countries in Western Europe outside its cities.

Castilian Spanish is the official language nationwide, but Spain has several other official regional languages that carry real cultural and political weight: Catalan in Catalonia and the Balearic Islands, Galician in Galicia, and Basque (Euskara) in the Basque Country and Navarre — a language with no known relation to any other on Earth. As a backpacker, Castilian Spanish (simply called "español" or "castellano" locally) will get you everywhere. English is widely spoken in tourist areas and by younger people, less so in small towns and rural areas, where a few basic Spanish phrases go a long way. Learning "por favor" and "gracias" is the bare minimum; learning a full sentence or two will regularly get you better service, bigger portions, and warmer conversations.

Spain uses the euro, which makes budgeting refreshingly simple if you're coming from the UK, US, or anywhere outside the eurozone — no confusing exchange rate mental maths required. Climate varies enormously by region and is one of the most important things to plan around. The interior (Madrid, Castile) has a continental climate: brutally hot summers (July highs regularly exceed 35°C) and genuinely cold winters. The Mediterranean coast and the south (Valencia, Andalusia) are warm nearly year-round. The north coast (Galicia, Asturias, Cantabria, the Basque Country) is cooler, greener, and considerably wetter — bring a rain jacket whatever the season. May, June, and September are widely considered the sweet spot for backpackers: warm enough for the coast, cool enough for hiking and city walking, and without the crushing heat or the peak-season crowds of July and August.

Frequently Asked Questions

How much does a dorm bed cost?

A dorm bed at a good Spanish hostel runs roughly €15–€30 a night in 2026, depending heavily on city and season. Madrid and Barcelona sit at the top of that range, especially in summer — book ahead in July and August. Seville, Valencia, and smaller cities are usually €15–€22. Galicia and the smaller Camino towns can be cheaper still, sometimes €10–€15 outside peak Camino season (April–October).

What does a private room cost?

A private double room at a hostel, usually with a shared bathroom, typically costs €40–€75 a night, with Barcelona and Madrid at the higher end and smaller cities considerably less. Split between two people, this is often cheaper than two dorm beds and gets you a door that locks — worth it after a few nights of communal snoring.

How do I get around?

Spain has one of the best public transport networks in Europe, which is good news for backpackers — you genuinely don't need to hire a car here unless you want the freedom of remote villages and the Camino's quieter back roads.

Trains (Renfe): The AVE high-speed network connects major cities — Madrid to Barcelona in under 3 hours, Madrid to Seville in under 2.5 — at speeds that make the UK's rail network look faintly embarrassing. AVE tickets are pricier than buses but drop significantly if booked a few weeks ahead. Regional (Media Distancia) and local (Cercanías) trains are slower and considerably cheaper, and are how most budget backpackers move between smaller towns.

Buses (ALSA and others): The budget workhorse of Spanish backpacking. ALSA covers virtually the entire country, including places the trains don't reach, and a Madrid–Seville route can cost under €20 if booked in advance. Comfortable, reliable, and generally on time.

BlaBlaCar: Spain's long-distance ride-share app is enormous here and is how a large proportion of young Spaniards and backpackers get between cities cheaply and socially. Often the cheapest option on a given route, and a good way to meet locals.

Car hire: Only worth it for specific regions — rural Andalusia's white villages, the Camino's back roads, Galicia's coastline, or a Pyrenees road trip — where public transport thins out. Spain drives on the right. Fuel and tolls (many Spanish motorways are toll roads) add up, so factor that in versus a train ticket.

Walking and cycling: If you're doing any part of the Camino de Santiago, this is your transport for that stretch — see our dedicated advice on the Camino for planning a route.

Is Spain safe?

Yes, overwhelmingly. Violent crime against tourists is rare. The one genuine, well-documented risk is petty theft and scams in dense tourist areas — pickpocketing on Las Ramblas and the Barcelona metro, phone snatching around Sol in Madrid, and a handful of well-known street scams (fake "free" bracelets, rigged shell games, aggressive "signature for charity" approaches). Keep bags zipped and worn to the front on crowded metros, don't leave your phone on café tables, and treat overly friendly strangers near major landmarks with polite scepticism. See our full Staying Safe & Travelling Well guide for the details.

Best Value: May

Spain rewards visiting in the shoulder seasons far more than most Western European countries. April brings Semana Santa (Holy Week) processions across the south — spectacular, but book accommodation months ahead if you want to be in Seville or Málaga for it. May and June give you long, warm days without July and August's genuinely oppressive interior heat.

July and August are peak season everywhere — hot, crowded, and expensive on the coast, but also when Spain's festival calendar is at its most intense: San Fermín (the Running of the Bulls) in Pamplona in July, and countless smaller local ferias throughout summer.

September is arguably the best all-round month: the crowds thin, the sea is still warm from summer, and the interior heat has broken. Winter (November–March) is mild on the coasts and in Andalusia, genuinely cold in Madrid and the north, and is ski season in the Pyrenees and Sierra Nevada.

Things To Do In Spain

Spain rewards a slower kind of travel than a checklist of monuments — but there is, undeniably, a checklist of monuments, and it is a very good one. Here's the shortlist to build your trip around, plus what to do when the budget runs low and how to catch a live match while you're here.

The Big Hits

Sagrada Família, Barcelona: Antoni Gaudí's still-unfinished basilica, under construction since 1882 and now (finally) nearing completion. Nothing prepares you for the interior — coloured light pouring through stained glass onto a forest of stone columns that don't look like they were designed by anyone sane. Book timed-entry tickets online well ahead; queues without a booking can run hours. Entry from roughly €26.

The Alhambra, Granada: A Moorish palace-fortress complex on a hill above Granada, and one of the most beautiful buildings in the world — intricate Nasrid stucco, reflecting pools, and gardens that make the whole thing feel like it was built for a fairy tale rather than a sultan. Tickets are timed, limited in number, and sell out days or weeks ahead in summer. Book the moment your dates are fixed. Entry from roughly €19.

The Alcázar & Cathedral, Seville: A working royal palace with over a thousand years of Moorish, Gothic, and Renaissance layers, next to the largest Gothic cathedral in the world (climb the Giralda tower for the best view in Seville — no stairs, just a ramp originally built so the muezzin could ride a horse to the top). Combined entry from roughly €20.

The Camino de Santiago: Not a single site but an entire pilgrimage network converging on Santiago de Compostela in Galicia. You don't need to walk all 780km of the Camino Francés — plenty of backpackers do the last 100km from Sarria (the minimum distance for an official Compostela certificate), staying in cheap pilgrim albergues along the way for a fraction of hostel prices elsewhere. See our dedicated Camino advice for planning a route.

Adrenaline & Adventure

Surfing the Basque and Cantabrian coast: San Sebastián's Zurriola beach and the string of breaks along the Cantabrian coast make northern Spain one of Europe's best surf regions, with a proper beginner scene and board hire and lessons widely available. Two-hour beginner lessons run roughly €35–€45.

Canyoning in the Pyrenees: Sierra de Guara in Aragón is one of the best canyoning destinations in Europe — abseiling down waterfalls, jumping into pools, and swimming through narrow limestone gorges. Full-day guided trips run roughly €55–€75.

Skiing and hiking the Sierra Nevada and the Pyrenees: The Sierra Nevada near Granada is mainland Europe's southernmost ski resort — you can genuinely ski in the morning and be on a Mediterranean beach by afternoon in the right season. The Pyrenees offer serious multi-day hiking, with hut-to-hut routes rivalling the Alps at a fraction of the cost.

Free Things To Do

Park Güell (the free part), Barcelona: The monumental zone requires a ticket, but the majority of Gaudí's park — the wooded paths and viewpoints over the city — is free to wander.

Tapas crawls, everywhere: In much of Andalusia (Granada especially), ordering a drink still gets you a free tapa. It's not a tourist gimmick — it's simply how bars work, and stringing three or four bars together for an evening can be close to a free dinner.

Plaza Mayor and the free walking tours, Madrid: Every major Spanish city has a "free" walking tour (tip-based) that's an excellent, sociable first orientation to a new place and a good way to meet other backpackers on day one.

Watching the sunset from a rooftop, Seville or Granada: Several rooftop bars charge nothing beyond the price of a drink for views across a skyline of domes, towers, and — in Granada's case — the Alhambra lit up against the Sierra Nevada.

Any beach in Spain: Every beach in the country is public. The Mediterranean coast, the wild Atlantic beaches of Cádiz, and the north coast's surf beaches cost nothing to enjoy.

Live Sport

Football (La Liga, year-round, peak September–May): Watching a match at Camp Nou (Barcelona) or the Santiago Bernabéu (Real Madrid) is a genuine bucket-list experience for a huge number of backpackers, and tickets for non-Clásico fixtures are far more attainable than most people assume — general admission from roughly €40–€60 for a standard league match, considerably more for the biggest fixtures.

Running of the Bulls, Pamplona (July): The encierro during the San Fermín festival is one of the most famous — and most reckless — spectacles in Europe. Watching from a balcony or barrier is safe and spectacular; actually running with the bulls is a genuinely dangerous activity that sends people to hospital most years, and this site does not recommend it lightly.

Basque pelota and rowing regattas, Basque Country: Regional sports with serious local followings — a pelota match in a fronton court or a trainera (traditional rowing boat) regatta along the Basque coast is a genuine slice of local life most tourists never see.
